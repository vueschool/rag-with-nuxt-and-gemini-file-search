# The Bellwind and the Broken Veil

The map was wrong.

Or at least, that’s what Taren told himself as the inked lines twisted into shapes that no longer matched the hills before him. The parchment crackled in his grip, as if it, too, were nervous. Wind rolled over the violet grasslands, carrying with it the faint chime of unseen bells—a sound that meant he was getting close.

Close to what, he wasn’t entirely sure.

“Remind me,” he muttered, squinting at the horizon, “why we didn’t stay in the warm tavern with the honeyed bread?”

A voice answered from his satchel.

“Because you decided you wanted to be _legendary_,” said Luma, the small, glowing creature curled among his supplies. She stretched, her light flickering like a candle. “And legends rarely start with comfort. Or common sense.”

Taren sighed. “I hate when you’re right.”

“You love when I’m right.”

“I tolerate it.”

They continued on.

---

The land of Eryndor was not like other places. It didn’t sit still. Forests migrated like herds, mountains shifted their shoulders overnight, and rivers occasionally decided to flow backward out of spite. Maps were less tools and more… suggestions.

Which is why Taren had accepted the quest in the first place.

The Veilstone had gone missing.

Not stolen—no one was foolish enough to steal a relic that held together the thin boundary between worlds—but _lost_. Slipped, vanished, wandered. No one knew. But its absence had consequences. Strange creatures had begun appearing in villages. Time hiccupped. Reflections blinked independently of their owners.

And the longer the Veilstone remained missing, the thinner reality would become.

Taren wasn’t a hero. Not really. He was a courier, a decent climber, and a man who had once accidentally talked his way out of being eaten by a sentient swamp.

But he had something others didn’t: he could hear the Bellwind.

That strange chiming breeze now singing across the plains.

It was said the Bellwind only rang near places where reality wore thin.

Which meant he was getting closer.

---

By dusk, the violet grass gave way to a forest of glass.

Not trees _like_ glass. Trees _made_ of it.

Their trunks rose in clear, shimmering columns, branches splitting into fractal patterns that caught the fading sunlight and shattered it into a thousand rainbows. Leaves chimed softly as they brushed together, each one a thin pane that reflected not the forest—but something else entirely.

Taren stepped carefully.

“Don’t touch anything,” Luma warned.

“I wasn’t going to—”

A branch brushed his shoulder.

For a moment, the world blinked.

He wasn’t in the forest anymore.

He stood in a grand hall, cloaked figures whispering around him. A crown lay in his hands—heavy, wrong. Someone knelt before him, eyes filled with fear.

Then—

The forest snapped back into place.

Taren staggered.

“Okay,” he said, breathing hard. “Definitely not touching anything.”

“Those are possibility echoes,” Luma said quietly. “Fragments of what could be. Or what almost was.”

“Great. Love that. Let’s not explore those further.”

They moved faster after that.

---

At the heart of the forest stood a ruin.

It shouldn’t have been there.

The stones were old—older than the forest, older than the land itself. Carvings spiraled across their surfaces in patterns that hurt to look at too long. The Bellwind roared here, no longer gentle, but urgent.

Taren approached the entrance.

“Tell me we’re not going in there,” he said.

“We are absolutely going in there,” Luma replied.

“Of course we are.”

Inside, the air was still.

Too still.

The sound of the wind vanished the moment he crossed the threshold, replaced by a deep, humming silence. The kind that pressed against your ears and made your thoughts feel… louder.

At the center of the chamber, on a pedestal of black stone, sat the Veilstone.

It was smaller than he expected.

A smooth, pale orb, barely the size of his fist. It glowed faintly, like moonlight trapped in glass. But as he stepped closer, he felt it—not as heat or cold, but as _pressure_. As if the world itself leaned toward it.

“Found it,” he whispered.

“Not so fast,” Luma said.

The shadows moved.

They peeled away from the walls, gathering into a shape that wasn’t quite solid. Tall. Too tall. Its edges blurred, as if it couldn’t decide where it ended.

“You should not be here,” it said, its voice layered, like many voices speaking at once.

Taren froze.

“Okay,” he said slowly. “Fair. Counterpoint—I _am_ here.”

Luma groaned. “Not the time.”

The figure stepped forward.

“The Veilstone is unmoored. It cannot return. Not now.”

“Why not?” Taren asked, surprising himself.

“Because it has seen too much.”

Taren blinked. “It’s a rock.”

“It is a _boundary_,” the figure replied. “And boundaries remember what they separate.”

The air warped.

Suddenly, Taren saw it—

Worlds layered over each other. Strange skies. Impossible cities. Creatures that defied shape and logic. The Veilstone pulsed, holding it all apart, barely.

And it was… cracking.

“You can’t just leave it here,” Taren said. “Things are already slipping through.”

“Yes,” the figure said. “And if it returns as it is, the fracture will spread.”

Silence stretched.

Taren looked at the Veilstone.

Then at his hands.

Then at Luma.

“...I have a bad idea,” he said.

“I hate your bad ideas,” Luma replied.

“They usually work.”

“They usually almost kill us.”

“Details.”

He stepped forward.

“Wait—” Luma started.

Taren grabbed the Veilstone.

Pain exploded through him.

Not physical—not exactly. It was like holding every version of himself at once. Every choice. Every path not taken. The crown. The swamp. The tavern he _didn’t_ stay in.

The fracture ran through him now.

He gasped.

“Then… fix it,” he choked.

The figure watched.

“You would bind yourself to the boundary?”

Taren laughed weakly. “I was aiming for ‘legendary,’ right?”

The light intensified.

Luma darted forward, her glow wrapping around his hand.

“Idiot,” she whispered. “You’re not doing this alone.”

Together, they held it.

Slowly—painfully—the fracture began to mend.

Not perfectly.

But enough.

The worlds pulled apart again, the pressure easing.

The Veilstone dimmed.

And Taren collapsed.

---

When he woke, the forest was gone.

He lay in a field of ordinary green grass under an ordinary blue sky.

Luma hovered nearby, dimmer than before, but smiling.

“You didn’t die,” she said.

“High bar,” Taren muttered.

He sat up.

In his hand, he still held the Veilstone.

But now, it was warm.

Steady.

“And now?” he asked.

Luma shrugged. “Now you carry it. Keeper of the boundary. Walker of the thin places.”

Taren groaned. “That sounds like a lot of responsibility.”

“It is.”

He looked at the horizon.

The wind chimed softly.

“…Do we at least get honeyed bread first?”

Luma grinned.

“Now _that_,” she said, “is a legendary idea.”

And so they walked on—into a world that would never quite sit still again, with a boundary that would always need tending, and a story that had only just begun.
